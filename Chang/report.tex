\section{ Chapter 3}
\subsection{Introduction}

The Hénon map is a classical two-dimensional dissipative map which, for the standard parameter values, produces a numerically observed strange attractor \cite{Henon1976}. Its dynamics combines area contraction with local stretching and folding, making it a useful model for studying the geometric mechanisms behind strange attractors.

This part of the report focuses on the tangent dynamics of the classical Hénon map. Instead of trying to describe the full attractor exactly, this part studies how infinitesimal perturbations evolve along long numerically generated orbits. The main objects are derivative products along the orbit, from which finite-time Lyapunov exponents and finite-time stable--unstable angles are computed.

These two diagnostics measure complementary aspects of the same geometric picture. Finite-time Lyapunov exponents measure local stretching and contraction rates, while stable--unstable angles measure the separation between the corresponding finite-time tangent directions. Together, they give a numerical description of the tangent geometry on the sampled attractor.

The aim is not to prove that the classical Hénon attractor is uniformly or non-uniformly hyperbolic. Instead, the computations are interpreted as finite-time diagnostics. Positive average expansion together with area contraction supports the expected dissipative stretching mechanism, while very small stable--unstable angles indicate that a uniform lower bound on directional separation is not numerically supported. This provides a geometric and numerical viewpoint that connects naturally with the wider themes of strange attractors, Lyapunov exponents, invariant measures, entropy, and fractal dimension.

\subsection{Theoretical background}
\label{sec:theoretical-model}


\subsubsection{Attractors and numerical samples}
\label{subsec:invariant-sets}

Let \(f:M\to M\) be a continuous map on a metric space \(M\). A set
\(\Lambda\subset M\) is called invariant if
\(f(\Lambda)=\Lambda,\) and positively invariant if
\(
f(\Lambda)\subset \Lambda.
\)

A compact region \(N\subset M\) is called a trapping region if \(f(N)\subset \operatorname{int}(N).\) In this case the forward images form a nested sequence \(N\supset f(N)\supset f^2(N)\supset \cdots,\) and the associated attracting set is \(\Lambda=\bigcap_{k\geq 0} f^k(N).\)

The basin of attraction of \(\Lambda\) is
\(W^s(\Lambda) = \{x\in M:\operatorname{dist}(f^n(x),\Lambda)\to 0
\text{ as } n\to\infty\}.\) 

The classical Hénon attractor is treated in this report as a numerically observed attracting set. If \(N\) is a trapping region, then repeated application of the map leaves a limiting invariant set \(\Lambda=\bigcap_{k\geq 0} f^k(N),\)
which is the set approached by orbits starting in its basin of attraction \(W^s(\Lambda)\). In the computations below, we do not attempt to construct \(\Lambda\) exactly. Instead, we iterate one initial condition for a long time, discard the transient part, and use the remaining orbit points as a finite sample of the observed attractor. This is the numerical object on which the finite-time Lyapunov exponents and stable--unstable angle diagnostics are computed. In this sense, the attractor is called strange because it combines attraction with sensitive dependence on initial conditions and complicated geometry \cite{Robinson1999}.





\subsubsection{Invariant measures and typical orbits}

\begin{definition}[Invariant and ergodic measure]
Let \(f:X\to X\) be a measurable map on a metric space \(X\). A probability measure \(\mu\) on \(X\) is called \(f\)-invariant if \(\mu(f^{-1}(A))=\mu(A)\)
for every measurable set \(A\subset X\).

An \(f\)-invariant probability measure \(\mu\) is called ergodic if every \(f\)-invariant measurable set has either measure \(0\) or measure \(1\).
\end{definition}

Invariant measures describe the statistical distribution of orbits. Ergodicity means, informally, that the system cannot be decomposed into two smaller invariant statistical components of positive measure. Thus, for an ergodic measure, typical orbits have the same statistical behaviour with respect to that measure.

\begin{theorem}[Birkhoff ergodic theorem, ergodic probability case]
Let \((X,\mathcal B,\mu)\) be a probability space, and let \(f:X\to X\) be a \(\mu\)-preserving ergodic map. If 
\(\varphi\in L^1(\mu)\), then for \(\mu\)-almost every \(x\in X\),
\[
\frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=0}^{N-1}\varphi(f^j(x))
\to
\int_X \varphi d\mu
\qquad
\text{as } N\to\infty .
\]
\end{theorem}


This theorem explains why a single long orbit can carry statistical information about the system. The left-hand side is a time average along one orbit, while the right-hand side is a space average with respect to the invariant measure. This is the sense in which ergodic theory studies typical orbits rather than exact pointwise descriptions of every orbit \cite{RasmussenErgodic2017,ColoniusKliemann2014}.

\begin{definition}[Empirical measure of a finite orbit]
Given a finite orbit segment
\(
x,f(x),\ldots,f^{m-1}(x),
\)
the associated empirical measure is
\[
\mu_m=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}\delta_{f^j(x)},\]
where \(\delta_y\) denotes the Dirac measure at \(y\).
\end{definition}

In numerical computations, after discarding a transient, a long orbit is treated as a finite sample of the observed attracting set. The empirical measure \(\mu_m\) records how often the orbit visits different regions of the attractor. If the orbit is typical for a natural invariant measure, then \(\mu_m\) gives a numerical approximation of the statistics of that measure.

\begin{remark}[Physical and SRB measures]
For dissipative chaotic attractors, the relevant invariant measure is often expected to be a physical or SRB measure. Informally, such a measure describes the statistics observed by typical initial conditions in the basin of attraction. In this report, we do not prove the existence or uniqueness of an SRB measure for the classical Hénon attractor. We only use this viewpoint to justify treating long post-transient orbits as numerical samples of the observed attractor.
\end{remark}

This measure-theoretic viewpoint explains why later asymptotic statements, such as Oseledets' theorem, are interpreted as almost-everywhere statements with respect to an invariant measure.




\subsubsection{Tangent dynamics and derivative cocycle}

\begin{definition}[Tangent vector and tangent space in Euclidean space]
Let \(p\in \mathbb R^n\). 

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item A tangent vector at \(p\) is a pair \(v_p = (p, v)\), $v\in \mathbb R^n$. The point \(p\) is called the base point of the tangent vector, and \(v\) is its direction vector. 

    \item The tangent space at \(p\), denoted by \(T_p\mathbb R^n\), is the set of all
    tangent vectors based at \(p\):
    \[
    T_p\mathbb R^n
    =
    \{(p,v):v\in\mathbb R^n\} \cong \mathbb R^n.
    \]
    
\end{enumerate}

\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Derivative and linearisation in Euclidean space]
Let \(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^m\) be a \(C^1\) map. The derivative of \(f\)
at \(p\in\mathbb R^n\) is the linear map
\(
Df_p:T_p\mathbb R^n\to T_{f(p)}\mathbb R^m
\)
with
\(
Df_p(p,v)=(f(p),Df(p)v),
\)
where \(Df(p)\) is the Jacobian matrix of \(f\) at \(p\).

\(Df_p\) gives the first-order approximation
\(
f(p+v)=f(p)+Df(p)v+o(\|v\|)\) as \( \|v\|\to 0.
\)
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Derivative cocycle]
Let \(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\) be a \(C^1\) map. For \(k\geq 1\), define
\[
Df_p^k := D(f^k)_p : T_p\mathbb R^n \to T_{f^k(p)}\mathbb R^n .
\]
By the chain rule,
\[
Df_p^k
=
Df_{f^{k-1}(p)}
Df_{f^{k-2}(p)}
\cdots
Df_{f(p)}
Df_p\].


The family
\(
(p,k)\mapsto Df_p^k
\)
is called the derivative cocycle over \(f\). It satisfies the cocycle identity
\[
Df_p^{k+\ell}
=
Df_{f^\ell(p)}^k Df_p^\ell
\qquad
\text{for all } k,\ell\geq 0.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Negative iterates]
If \(f\) is a \(C^1\) diffeomorphism, then negative iterates are also defined. For
\(k\geq 1\), we write
\[
Df_p^{-k}:=D(f^{-k})_p:T_p\mathbb R^n\to T_{f^{-k}(p)}\mathbb R^n .
\]
Here \(Df_p^{-k}\) denotes the derivative of the inverse iterate \(f^{-k}\) at
\(p\).
\end{remark}



Let \(p_j=f^j(p)\). After identifying \(T_{p_j}\mathbb R^n\) with \(\mathbb R^n\), an infinitesimal perturbation evolves by \(
v_{j+1}=Df(p_j)v_j.\) Hence after \(k\) steps,
\(
v_k=Df_p^k v_0
=
Df(p_{k-1})\cdots Df(p_0)v_0.
\)
Thus the nonlinear orbit induces a linearised perturbation dynamics along the orbit.




\subsubsection{Lyapunov exponents and Oseledets' theorem}

\begin{definition}[Lyapunov exponent]
Let \(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\) be differentiable and let \(x\in \mathbb R^n\). For a nonzero tangent vector \(v\in T_x\mathbb R^n \), the Lyapunov exponent in the direction \(v\) is
\(\lambda(x,v) =
\lim_{n\to\infty}
\frac{1}{n}\log \|D(f^n)_x v\|,
\)
when the limit exists.
\end{definition}

Lyapunov exponents measure the asymptotic exponential growth or contraction of tangent vectors under the derivative cocycle. A positive exponent corresponds to exponential expansion in the corresponding tangent direction, while a negative exponent corresponds to exponential contraction. The existence of these limits is by Oseledets'
multiplicative ergodic theorem for typical points.





\begin{theorem}[Oseledets' theorem for \(C^1\) diffeomorphisms on \(\mathbb R^2\)]
Let \(f:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R^2\) be a \(C^1\) diffeomorphism, let
\(\Lambda\subset\mathbb R^2\) be a compact \(f\)-invariant set, and let \(\mu\)
be an ergodic \(f\)-invariant probability measure supported on \(\Lambda\).
Assume
\[
\log^+\|Df\|,\ \log^+\|Df^{-1}\|\in L^1(\mu),
\]
where \(\log^+(t)=\max\{\log t,0\}\). Then there exists an \(f\)-invariant set
\(\Lambda_0\subset\Lambda\) with \(\mu(\Lambda_0)=1\) such that, for every
\(p\in\Lambda_0\), there are Lyapunov exponents \(\alpha_1>\alpha_2>\cdots>\alpha_\ell
\)
and a measurable invariant splitting
\[
T_p\mathbb R^2
=
E_1(p)\oplus\cdots\oplus E_\ell(p),
\qquad
Df_pE_i(p)=E_i(f(p)),
\]
such that for every \(0\neq v\in E_i(p)\),
\[
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac1k\log\|Df_p^k v\|
=
\alpha_i.
\]

Moreover, if \(\sigma_1(Df_p^k)\geq\sigma_2(Df_p^k)>0\) are the singular
values of \(Df_p^k\), then
\[
\lim_{k\to\infty}\frac1k\log\sigma_j(Df_p^k),
\qquad j=1,2,
\]
exist and give the Lyapunov exponents counted with multiplicity.
\end{theorem}

In our setting, the integrability condition is automatically satisfied
because \(Df\) and \(Df^{-1}\) are continuous and hence bounded on \(\Lambda\).
The theorem justifies two things. First, Lyapunov exponents are well-defined
asymptotic tangent growth rates for \(\mu\)-almost every point. Second, these
growth rates can be obtained from singular values of the derivative cocycle.

This motivates the finite-time Lyapunov exponents used numerically:
\[
\lambda_j^{(k)}(p)
=
\frac1k\log\sigma_j(Df_p^k),
\qquad j=1,2.
\]
They are finite-window versions of the asymptotic Oseledets quantities. In this
report, they are used as numerical diagnostics of stretching and contraction, not
as proofs that the limiting exponents exist at every sampled point.

\begin{remark}[Relation with stable and unstable directions]
If the two Lyapunov exponents satisfy \(\alpha_1>0>\alpha_2\), the Oseledets
splitting gives an expanding direction \(E_1(p)\) and a contracting direction
\(E_2(p)\) for \(\mu\)-almost every \(p\). This is weaker than uniform
hyperbolicity, where the stable and unstable directions are defined at every point,
vary continuously, and satisfy uniform contraction and expansion estimates.
\end{remark}



\subsubsection{Hyperbolicity and uniform transversality}

\begin{definition}[Hyperbolic invariant set]
Let \(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\) be a \(C^1\) diffeomorphism, and let
\(\Lambda\subset \mathbb R^n\) be a compact invariant set. We say \(\Lambda\) is a hyperbolic invariant set if for every \(p\in\Lambda\),
the tangent space \(T_p\mathbb R^n\) admits a direct-sum decomposition
\[
T_p\mathbb R^n
=
E_p^s\oplus E_p^u,
\]
such that:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item The splitting is invariant under the derivative:
    \(
    Df_p(E_p^s)=E_{f(p)}^s,
    Df_p(E_p^u)=E_{f(p)}^u.
    \)

    \item There exist constants \(C\geq 1\) and \(0<\lambda<1\), independent of
    \(p\in\Lambda\), such that for all \(k\geq 0\),
    \[
    \|Df_p^k v^s\|
    \leq
    C\lambda^k\|v^s\|,
    \qquad
    v^s\in E_p^s,
    \]
    and
    \[
    \|Df_p^{-k} v^u\|
    \leq
    C\lambda^k\|v^u\|,
    \qquad
    v^u\in E_p^u.
    \]
\end{enumerate}

The subspaces \(E_p^s\) and \(E_p^u\) are called the stable and unstable
subspaces at \(p\), respectively.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Uniform versus non-uniform hyperbolicity]
In the above definition, the constants \(C\geq 1\) and \(0<\lambda<1\) are independent of the base point \(p\in\Lambda\). Thus a hyperbolic invariant set in this sense is uniformly hyperbolic.

This is different from non-uniform hyperbolicity. In the non-uniform setting, one usually works with an invariant probability measure and assumes that the Lyapunov exponents are nonzero for \(\mu\)-almost every point. The stable--unstable splitting is then typically measurable and defined only almost everywhere, and the contraction and expansion estimates need not be controlled by constants uniform over the whole set \cite{EckmannRuelle1985,Barreira2012}.
\end{remark}


\begin{definition}[Stable and unstable directions in dimension two]
Let \(f:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb R^2\) be a \(C^1\) diffeomorphism and
\(\Lambda\subset\mathbb R^2\) be a hyperbolic invariant set. If for each \(p\in\Lambda\),
\(
\dim E_p^s=\dim E_p^u=1,
\)
then \(E_p^s\) and \(E_p^u\) are called the stable and unstable directions at
\(p\), respectively.


\end{definition}

In \(\mathbb R^2\), if both \(E_p^s\) and \(E_p^u\) are one-dimensional, they are lines in \(T_p\mathbb R^2\cong \mathbb R^2.\)
The stable direction \(E_p^s\) consists of infinitesimal directions contracted under forward iteration. The unstable direction \(E_p^u\) consists of infinitesimal directions expanded under forward iteration, equivalently contracted under backward iteration.

If \(E_p^s\) and \(E_p^u\) are spanned by unit vectors \(e_p^s\) and \(e_p^u\), respectively, the angle between the two directions is
\[
\theta(p)
=
\angle(E_p^s,E_p^u)
=
\arccos\left(\left|\langle e_p^s,e_p^u\rangle\right|\right)
\in [0,\pi/2].
\]
The absolute value is used because the angle is between one-dimensional subspaces.


\begin{remark}
The directions \(E_p^s\) and \(E_p^u\) are not generally eigenvectors of \(Df(p)\). The derivative maps
\(
Df_p:T_p\mathbb R^2\to T_{f(p)}\mathbb R^2,
\)
so it transports directions from one tangent space to the next along the orbit. At a fixed point, these directions coincide with the stable and unstable eigenspaces of \(Df(p)\). At a periodic point of period \(q\), they correspond to the stable and unstable eigenspaces of \(D(f^q)(p)\).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Basic properties of hyperbolic sets]
Let \(\Lambda\) be a hyperbolic invariant set for a \(C^1\) diffeomorphism
\(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R^n\). Then the following properties hold:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item The stable and unstable subspaces vary continuously with the base point:
    \(
    p\mapsto E_p^s\), 
    \(p\mapsto E_p^u.
    \)

    \item The stable and unstable subspaces are uniformly transverse:
    \(
    \inf_{p\in\Lambda}\angle(E_p^s,E_p^u)>0.\)
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}


Property (2) is the theoretical basis for the angle diagnostic. Indeed, for a uniformly hyperbolic set, the subspaces \(E_p^s\) and \(E_p^u\) depend continuously on \(p\). Hence the angle function
\[
p\mapsto \angle(E_p^s,E_p^u)
\]
is continuous on the compact set \(\Lambda\). Since \(E_p^s\cap E_p^u=\{0\}\) for every \(p\in\Lambda\), this angle is strictly positive at every point. By compactness, it therefore has a positive minimum:
\[
\inf_{p\in\Lambda}\angle(E_p^s,E_p^u)>0.
\]
Thus, in \(\mathbb R^2\), uniform hyperbolicity implies that the stable and unstable directions never become tangent and cannot become arbitrarily close anywhere on \(\Lambda\).

This justifies the numerical angle diagnostic used later. If the sampled attractor behaved like a uniformly hyperbolic invariant set, the finite-time stable and unstable directions would be expected to remain separated by a positive lower bound. Therefore, persistent very small finite-time angles do not prove non-uniform hyperbolicity, but they indicate that the computation does not support a uniform lower bound on directional separation.



\begin{remark}[the Smale horseshoe]
The Smale horseshoe is a standard uniformly hyperbolic model of stretching and folding \cite{Robinson1999}. It provides a clean contrast with the classical Hénon attractor: the horseshoe has a rigorously defined hyperbolic invariant set and symbolic coding, whereas the Hénon attractor considered here is studied through finite-time numerical diagnostics along long forward orbits. 
\end{remark}






\subsubsection{The Hénon map}
\label{sec:Hénon-map}

    \begin{definition}[Hénon map]
    For parameters \(A,B\in\mathbb R\), define
    \[
    F_{AB}(x,y) = (A-By-x^2,x).
    \]
    \end{definition}

    For the classical parameters $A=1.4$, $B=-0.3$, we write $ F:=F_{1.4,-0.3}$. Numerical iteration suggests that long forward orbits of \(F\) approach a thin attracting set, usually called the classical Hénon attractor \cite{Henon1976,Robinson1999}.

   
    The derivative of the Hénon map is
    \[
    DF_{AB}(x,y) =
    \begin{pmatrix}
    -2x & -B\\
    1 & 0
    \end{pmatrix},
    \]
    and hence $\det DF_{AB}(x,y)=B$. Thus, for \(|B|<1\), the map contracts area by the constant factor \(|B|\) at each step. In particular, since \(B=-0.3\neq0\), the classical map \(F=F_{1.4,-0.3}\) is invertible.

    
    \begin{remark}[Relation with the common Hénon convention]
    Some texts use
    \[
    H_{a,b}(x,y)=(1-ax^2+y,bx).
    \] The two conventions are linearly conjugate after rescaling coordinates.
    The classical parameters \(a=1.4\), \(b=0.3\) correspond to the convention above with $A=1.4$ and $B=-0.3$.
    \end{remark}

    The relevant geometric mechanism is the interaction between folding, dissipation, and tangent stretching. The nonlinear term \(-x^2\) folds regions in the plane, while the constant determinant gives uniform area contraction when \(|B|<1\). At the same time, individual tangent directions may still be expanded, and this is measured by the derivative cocycle  $D(F^k)(z_0)
    =
    DF(z_{k-1})\cdots DF(z_0).$
    Thus the classical Hénon map is a natural setting for studying finite-time stretching rates and finite-time stable--unstable direction separation on a numerically observed strange attractor.
    
    Depending on the parameters, the maximal invariant set can have different geometric forms. For fixed \(B\), sufficiently large \(A\) gives a horseshoe-type structure, while the classical parameters produce the numerically observed strange attractor studied here \cite{Robinson1999}. This makes the Hénon map a useful example for relating Lyapunov exponents, tangent directions, and fractal geometry.
   
    

\subsection{Numerical tangent diagnostics for the classical Hénon attractor}

The numerical part studies the tangent dynamics along long forward orbits of the classical Hénon map. The aim is to understand how the observed strange attractor combines expansion, contraction, and changing tangent directions. Two diagnostics are used: finite-time Lyapunov exponents measure stretching and contraction rates, while finite-time stable--unstable angles measure the separation of the numerically approximated tangent directions.

These computations are not intended to prove hyperbolicity. Instead, they provide finite-time evidence about the local geometric mechanisms on the sampled attractor.

\subsubsection{Finite-time quantities}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Derivative products along an orbit:}
    For an orbit \(z_{j+1}=F(z_j)\), we compute the finite derivative product
    \(D(F^n)(z_j)=DF(z_{j+n-1})\cdots DF(z_j).
    \) This matrix maps tangent perturbations at \(z_j\) to tangent perturbations at \(z_{j+n}\) over a finite orbit segment of length \(n\).

    \item \textbf{Singular values and finite-time Lyapunov exponents}

        Let \(L\in\mathbb R^{2\times 2}\). The singular values of \(L\) are \(\sigma_1(L)\geq \sigma_2(L)\geq 0,\)
        where \(\sigma_i(L)^2\) are the eigenvalues of \(L^TL\), showing the maximal stretching factors of the linear map \(L\) in orthogonal directions.
        
        
        For the derivative product \(D(F^n)(z_j)\), define
        \[
        \lambda_i^{(n)}(z_j)
        =
        \frac1n\log\sigma_i(D(F^n)(z_j)),
        \qquad i=1,2.
        \]
        \(\lambda_1^{(n)}(z_j)\) measures the strongest average expansion rate over the finite segment, while \(\lambda_2^{(n)}(z_j)\) measures the strongest average contraction rate.
        
        As explained in the theoretical background, Oseledets' theorem motivates this definition by relating asymptotic Lyapunov exponents to the limits of \(\frac1n\log \sigma_i(D(F^n)(z)).\) In the numerical part, we only use the finite-time quantities \(\lambda_i^{(n)}(z_j)\). They should be interpreted as finite-window diagnostics of tangent stretching, not as proof that the asymptotic Lyapunov exponents exist at every sampled point.
        
        \begin{remark}[Area contraction for the Hénon map]
        For the Hénon map, \(\det DF_{AB}(x,y)=B\), so that \(\det D(F^n)(z_j)=B^n.\)
        Since the product of the singular values equals \(|\det D(F^n)(z_j)|\), we obtain
        \(
        \sigma_1(D(F^n)(z_j))\sigma_2(D(F^n)(z_j))= |B|^n.\) Therefore \(\lambda_1^{(n)}(z_j)+\lambda_2^{(n)}(z_j)=\log |B|.\)
        For the classical parameters, this gives
        \(
        \lambda_1^{(n)}(z_j)+\lambda_2^{(n)}(z_j)=\log 0.3<0.\)
        Thus the total area growth is always contracting, even when one tangent direction expands. Numerically, it is therefore enough to compute \(\lambda_1^{(n)}(z_j)\) from the largest singular value and then set \(\lambda_2^{(n)}(z_j)=\log|B|-\lambda_1^{(n)}(z_j).\)
        \end{remark}

        \iffalse
        \begin{remark}[Finite-time interpretation]
        The dependence of \(\lambda_i^{(n)}(z_j)\) on both the base point \(z_j\) and the step length \(n\) describes how stretching and contraction vary along the sampled attractor.
        \end{remark}
        \fi


    
    \item \textbf{Finite-time stable and unstable directions and angles.}

    The angle diagnostic is based on the following hyperbolic intuition. Suppose first that a genuine hyperbolic splitting \(T_z\mathbb R^2=E_z^s\oplus E_z^u \) exists. Then a generic tangent vector has a nonzero component in the unstable direction, and forward iteration amplifies this component relative to the stable one. Therefore, after repeated normalisation, forward propagation tends to align with the unstable direction. Similarly, backward propagation tends to align with the stable direction.

    In the numerical computation, we use this mechanism only as a finite-time approximation procedure. We do not assume that the exact spaces \(E_z^s\) and \(E_z^u\) have already been proved to exist on the classical Hénon attractor.

    \begin{definition}[Finite-time propagated directions]
    Let \(N\geq 1\), and let $ z_{-N},z_{-N+1},\ldots,z_0,\ldots,z_N$ be an orbit segment with \(F(z_j)=z_{j+1}\) for $j=-N,...,-1, 0,1,...,N-1$.

    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
        \item Choose a nonzero initial vector \(u_{-N}\in T_{z_{-N}}\mathbb R^2\), and define inductively by
            \[
            u_{j+1}
            =
            \frac{DF(z_j)u_j}{\|DF(z_j)u_j\|},
            \qquad
            j=-N,\ldots,-1.
            \]
        Then the unit vector \(u_0^{(N)}\) is used as the finite-time unstable direction at \(z_0\).

        \item Choose a nonzero terminal vector \(s_N\in T_{z_N}\mathbb R^2\), and define inductively by
            \[
            s_{j-1}
            =
            \frac{DF(z_{j-1})^{-1}s_j}{\|DF(z_{j-1})^{-1}s_j\|},
            \qquad
            j=N,\ldots,1.
            \]
        Then the unit vector \(s_0^{(N)}\) is used as the finite-time stable direction at \(z_0\).
        \end{enumerate}
    \end{definition}

    The finite-time stable--unstable angle is then defined by
    \[
    \theta^{(N)}(z_0)
    =
    \arccos
    \left(
    \left|
    \left\langle s_0^{(N)},u_0^{(N)}\right\rangle
    \right|
    \right)
    \in[0,\pi/2].
    \]

    Note that singular values are used only for stretching rates, while the angle diagnostic uses propagated tangent directions.

    For a uniformly hyperbolic invariant set, the stable and unstable directions are uniformly transverse:
    \( \inf_{z\in\Lambda}\theta(z)>0. \)
    Therefore, very small values of
    \( \theta^{(N)}(z) \)
    along a numerical orbit suggest small finite-time directional separation or possible near-tangencies.

    

\end{enumerate}
    
    
    
    







\subsubsection{Numerical results and interpretation}

We now apply the numerical diagnostics to the classical Hénon map. The orbit was generated from \(z_0=(0,0)\), with \(10^4\) burn-in iterates
discarded and \(10^5\) points retained. The finite-time Lyapunov exponents were
computed at \(5000\) sampled base points on the orbit. 


\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Finite-time Lyapunov exponents.}
    
    The largest finite-time Lyapunov exponent was computed from the largest
    singular value of \(D(F^n)(z)\). The second exponent was obtained from
    \(
    \lambda_1^{(n)}(z)+\lambda_2^{(n)}(z)
    =
    \log |B|
    =
    \log 0.3.\)
    A long-orbit repeated-normalisation estimate gives \(\lambda_1\approx 0.4214\) and \(\lambda_2\approx -1.6254
    \).

    \item \textbf{Finite-time stable--unstable angles.}
    The second diagnostic examines whether the propagated finite-time stable and unstable directions remain separated. For the classical Hénon parameter, these directions are interpreted only as finite-time numerical approximations, not as a proved invariant splitting.

    Rigorous non-uniform hyperbolicity results are known for certain Hénon parameter sets, such as the Benedicks--Carleson parameters, but these results do not directly apply to the classical parameter considered here \cite{BenedicksCarleson1991, GaliasTucker2015}. Therefore, the computed directions are interpreted only as finite-time approximations along the sampled orbit, not as a proof of global hyperbolicity.

(a) indicates that the direction approximation stabilises rapidly as the propagation length \(N\) increases: from about \(N=10\) onwards, the \(1\%\) quantile, \(5\%\) quantile and median change very little. This suggests that the computed directions are not mainly determined by the arbitrary initial tangent vectors used in the propagation.

    Overall, the angle diagnostic gives a mixed conclusion. Most sampled points have well-separated finite-time directions, which is consistent with hyperbolic-type tangent behaviour on a large part of the attractor. However, the persistent small-angle lower tail means that the computation does not support a numerical uniform lower bound on directional separation. The distribution of small-angle points suggests that the loss of transversality is likely to be tied to specific folded regions of the attractor. Thus the angle diagnostic supports a picture of non-uniform finite-time tangent geometry: directional separation is typical, but not uniform across the sampled attractor. This should be interpreted as non-uniform finite-time tangent behaviour, not as a proof of non-uniform hyperbolicity for the classical Hénon parameter.

    
    \item \textbf{Comparison between stretching and angle diagnostics.}

    The two diagnostics test different aspects of the same finite-time picture. The finite-time Lyapunov exponents measure the size of stretching and contraction, while the angle diagnostic measures the separation of the propagated stable and unstable directions. Together, they suggest average expansion with area contraction, but without numerical support for uniform directional separation across the whole sampled orbit.

\subsection{Connection with dimension}

The numerical Lyapunov exponent estimates also link with fractal dimension through the Kaplan--Yorke, or Lyapunov dimension \cite{EckmannRuelle1985}. For a two-dimensional dissipative map with
\(
\lambda_1>0,
\qquad
\lambda_1+\lambda_2<0,
\)
the Kaplan--Yorke estimate is
\[
D_{KY}
=
1+\frac{\lambda_1}{|\lambda_2|}.
\]
Using the numerical estimates $\lambda_1\approx 0.4214$, $\lambda_2\approx -1.6254$, we obtain
\(
D_{KY}\approx 1.259.
\)

This is consistent with the geometric picture of the Hénon attractor as a thin fractal set: one tangent direction expands on average, giving a dimension larger than \(1\), while the total area contraction keeps the dimension below \(2\). However, this should only be interpreted as a Lyapunov-dimension estimate. It is not a rigorous computation of Hausdorff dimension, box-counting dimension, or the dimension of an invariant measure.
